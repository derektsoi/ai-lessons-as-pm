The sharpest line in the lesson is the penthouse metaphor: "I couldn't see this gap because I was standing on it." It captures the core message — the invisibility of accumulated infrastructure to the person who built it — and creates immediate curiosity. It's a statement, not a description.

## Voice Check (SUCCESS Framework)

### Simple + Unexpected (Opening)

The opening paragraph — "Every PM on my team now has an AI learning OKR. It affects their performance review — salary and bonus." — is **Simple**: short sentences, no jargon, immediate stakes. The turn — "Then I checked the pull requests. Only Olivia had submitted anything outside of OKR work." — is **Unexpected**: the reader expects the OKR move to work; instead it reveals compliance masquerading as adoption. A reader seeing this in their inbox gets a clear promise: this person did the obvious thing, and it didn't work the way they expected. That's enough to keep reading.

**Verdict: Opening passes Simple + Unexpected.**

### Emotional + Stories (Middle)

The middle has several felt moments:
- "I couldn't see this gap because I was standing on it" — the penthouse metaphor is a felt realization, not just analysis
- "Obsession isn't a method" — this is an uncomfortable admission with emotional weight
- Olivia's SEO report journey (manual data pulling to branded interactive reports) is a concrete story with transformation arc
- "I asked her about it. She pointed to the SEO reports as the driver. She couldn't name the rest either." — honest, slightly uncomfortable, shared not-knowing
- The Feynman Test section is analysis, but it's grounded in personal failure ("it just caught me") which gives it emotional charge

The weakest emotional section is "The Bet" — it risks reading as pure reasoning about the decision to move fast. The fabrication anecdote (Claude inventing business logic, multi-agent review amplifying it) provides a concrete moment that saves it from pure abstraction.

**Verdict: Middle passes Emotional + Stories. The Olivia section and the Feynman catch are the strongest emotional beats.**

## Questions for the Author

1. **Does the title capture the sharpest insight?** "I Couldn't See the Gap Because I Was Standing on It" captures the invisibility of accumulated infrastructure. Alternative candidates: "Obsession Isn't a Method" (punchier but narrower — only covers the Feynman thread, not the infrastructure gap itself) or "The Gap Between Using AI and Using AI Well Is Invisible Infrastructure" (the scaffold title — accurate but reads like a description, not a hook).

2. **Does the opening give enough context without the scaffold?** The opening establishes: PM role, team with AI OKRs, performance review stakes, GitHub/Claude Code setup, compliance vs. adoption finding. A reader who hasn't read lessons 01-04 gets enough to follow. The "Lesson 04" cross-reference is removed. The one risk: the opening assumes the reader understands what "OKR" means. This audience (PMs, tech leaders reading about AI adoption) likely does, but it's worth flagging.

3. **The Role Shift section was the biggest restructure.** The scaffold had a full section on management philosophy — four years of org-building, losing patience with people who don't take initiative, the "not accountable for their growth" principle, then the scope clause. I compressed all of this into "The Bet" framing. The management backstory is now absent. If that was one of the lesson's key insights (not just context), it deserves more space.

4. **"Infrastructure building infrastructure"** — this phrase was cut from the weekly learning loop description. It's a strong conceptual line. Worth finding a place for it?
