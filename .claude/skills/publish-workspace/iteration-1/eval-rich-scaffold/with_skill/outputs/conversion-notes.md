# Conversion Notes — Lesson 01

## Title choice

Kept "A Working Demo Doesn't Let You Be Vague" from the existing Substack draft. It's still the sharpest line in the scaffold — it comes directly from the core insight section and captures the mechanism (demos force specificity) without overselling.

## Subtitle change

Changed from the previous draft's subtitle ("What I learned about decision-making when I started building product demos with Claude Code instead of writing PRDs") to something broader: "What happens to product development when the PM stops writing specs and starts building evidence." Reason: the original was too much of a description. The new one frames the broader tension without naming the tool in the subtitle (the tool shows up naturally in the body).

## What I included vs. the previous Substack draft

The existing `01-substack-draft.md` converted only the "Core Insight" section and "What I'm still figuring out." This scaffold has significantly more drafted prose — particularly the "What I Got Wrong" and "Where This Works" sections, which were fleshed out after the challenge round. I included them because they're honest, grounded, and make the article stronger:

- **"The risk I didn't expect"** — combines the judgment-is-higher-leverage insight with the technical-details scoping concern. These were separate bullets in the scaffold but belong together as one section about unintended consequences.
- **"Where this works — and where it probably doesn't"** — included because the scaffold itself flags that two projects isn't a general method. Acknowledging that directly makes the article more credible.
- **"It's both"** (the enjoyment admission) — moved into "What I'm still figuring out" as a closing beat. It's the kind of honesty that makes this voice distinctive.

## What I cut

- All `> Status:` lines
- All `> Think through:` prompt blocks (5 of them)
- "Raw Material & References" section (local file paths)
- "If Cross-Posting to Medium" section (internal planning)
- "The Lesson (Draft — Refine This)" section — the framings and meta-commentary about which framing is best. The article embodies the chosen framing; it doesn't need to show the work of arriving at it.
- "The Demo Owner Experiment" section — still in bullet/scaffold form, not enough prose to convert. The engineering-role framing is interesting but underdeveloped. Could be a future article.
- Italic editorial notes (e.g., "_Expand on: what happened when stakeholders saw the mockups_")
- The two-example breakdown under "What I Do Now" (Trending Merchants quarterly planning, Pokemon Shopping Agent) — the specific details from these examples are already woven into the core insight and "this isn't specific to AI products" sections with better context. Including them as a separate enumerated section would be redundant.

## Structural decisions

- Added `---` dividers between every major section for Substack visual breaks.
- The opening paragraph sets the scene (old workflow, its failures) and the second paragraph states the thesis. This follows the pattern from the previous draft but tightens the second paragraph to preview the "two halves" of the lesson — the power and the risk.
- Sections follow the scaffold's natural arc: discovery ("I didn't set out to...") -> mechanism (Claude Code's role) -> generalization (merchant pinning) -> naming it (decision compression) -> the risk -> boundaries -> open questions.
- Closing bio uses first name and is shorter than the previous draft's version. Dropped the "not theory" qualifier — the article demonstrates that without needing to say it.

## Voice notes

- Preserved the author's direct, specific style. No hedging language added.
- Kept "I don't have a replacement yet" and "it's both" as closing admissions — these are the article's most distinctive moments.
- Did not smooth over the tension between "this is powerful" and "this is risky." The article holds both without resolving them, which matches the scaffold's intent.
