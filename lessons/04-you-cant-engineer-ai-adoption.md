# I Spent Months Inspiring When I Should Have Just Set the Expectation

> Status: Scaffold — think-through (4 rounds) and challenge done, ready for drafting

## The Setup

- I'm a PM lead who went deep on Claude Code — 400 messages, 43 sessions, 10 projects in one week.
- I wanted the product team to work this way too.
- I tried demos, walkthroughs, pairing sessions, building tools. Some people adopted. Most didn't.
- I spent months thinking this was an adoption problem. I now believe it's a leadership problem — four layers deep.
- Caveat: this is observations from one team (~6-8 people), not a generalizable framework. I'm sharing what I saw.

---

## What I Tried (and What Happened)

### The inspiration approach

I showed people what Claude Code could do. I walked people through it multiple times. I built impressive demos. I made my own work visible. The implicit message was: "Look how powerful this is — you should want to use it."

Result: mostly nothing. People nodded politely and went back to their old workflow. I thought they were impressed but unmotivated. The truth was worse.

### The forcing approach

I assigned one PM a monthly SEO analysis task through Claude Code. She had no coding experience. She now runs it independently via a slash command — a full interactive report.

Result: the one person I actually forced is the strongest adopter. But I still can't fully explain why it worked for her. She doesn't know either. "Forcing" may have been necessary but not sufficient — something about her disposition plus the right task made it stick. I forced other PMs with similar tasks. They didn't adopt. One data point isn't a method.

### The pairing approach

The junior PM (QA background) was eager to try for months but didn't have the chance. I sat with him for 30 minutes, showed him agent teams, helped him set up basic skills. Now he's using Claude Code proactively.

Result: worked — but only because he was already motivated. The 30 minutes removed a setup barrier, not a motivation barrier.

### The self-starter

The data team lead — doesn't love coding — built a daily Markov chain contribution model analysis on a Raspberry Pi. Nobody forced him. He found his own problem and went after it. He doesn't need me at all.

He also challenged my approach to building tools that hide AI from users: "That doesn't help the team learn." He has a point — for PMs. For marketing, I still think abstracting AI away is the right call.

### The people I didn't push

- Senior PM with technical background: she's senior, doesn't listen to me, and I didn't force her. Her real problem isn't AI adoption — she wants to learn business fundamentals but is stuck in execution details. AI won't fix that.
- Marketing: scared of VS Code. I build tools for them (blog writer: type a command, get a WordPress post in 50 minutes). Different problem, different solution. They don't need to learn Claude Code — they need better output. (Caveat: I assumed they can't learn because they haven't seen VS Code. But the PM who adopted hadn't seen VS Code either. This assumption is untested.)
- Engineers: only ~7% at our company doing AI-assisted coding beyond tab completion (CTO confirmed). I haven't intervened yet — that's a separate initiative working through the CTO.

## What I Got Wrong (Four Layers)

_Note after challenge round: some of these layers were the right call at the time. The insight isn't that they were always wrong — it's recognizing when they stop being correct._

### Layer 0: The groundwork wasn't wasted — but I didn't recognize when it was done

I couldn't have set the expectation on day one. I hadn't proven to myself that AI-assisted PM work actually delivers. The 10 projects in a week, the PM's SEO report, the Pokemon agent spec — I was building the evidence base. If that work hadn't delivered, how could I ask anyone else to do it?

The months of "inspiration" were actually necessary groundwork: validating that this way of working produces better output, building concrete examples, figuring out what the standard looks like. I wasn't wasting time — I was doing the prerequisite work.

The groundwork also built the expertise I need to support others through adoption. Without having done the obsessive work myself, I couldn't troubleshoot when someone gets stuck, build the skills they need, or demonstrate what "good" looks like. The support depends on the expertise. This playbook only works for leaders willing to do the real work first — and I say that deliberately, not as a caveat. That's my leadership style. I can't ask people to do something I haven't done myself.

The mistake wasn't doing the groundwork. The mistake was not recognizing the transition point — the moment when the evidence was sufficient and it was time to shift from demonstrating to expecting. I stayed in "proving it" mode too long because proving it was comfortable and setting expectations was hard.

### Layer 1: I confused inspiration with leadership

Once the groundwork was done, I kept doing demos and walkthroughs. The implicit assumption: if they see how powerful this is, they'll want to use it.

That's not how change works. For people who already have the drive (the data lead, the junior PM), inspiration is unnecessary — they just need barriers removed. For everyone else, inspiration produced polite nods, not action.

A demo lets you be the inspiring leader. An expectation makes you accountable for the outcome. I chose the comfortable move.

### Layer 2: I avoided setting expectations because it's harder

If I say "all PMs must deliver analysis and demos through AI," then I own what happens when someone can't or won't. That turns an adoption problem into a performance problem. I wasn't ready for that conversation.

This is the uncomfortable truth: I was choosing the comfortable leadership move (inspire) over the effective one (set expectations and support the transition).

### Layer 3: I withheld the reason — so the team thought it was my hobby

The PM who adopted told me something I didn't want to hear: the team believes AI adoption is my personal agenda. Mine and the data lead's. They think we push it because we like it.

She was right that I hadn't been transparent. It wasn't until that lunch conversation that I realized I'd never shared the context. Our CEO, COO, and CTO are all aligned that AI-assisted work is the direction for the company. This isn't a trend piece or a Silicon Valley import — our leadership has decided this is where we're going. But I never told the team that. I kept the industry pressure and leadership alignment to myself because I didn't want to scare them.

The result: without the "why," every demo and walkthrough reinforced the narrative that this was Derek's hobby. The team was being polite, not resistant. They didn't see the urgency because I never shared it.

### The lunch that made it concrete

At that same lunch, we talked about her career trajectory. We agreed she'd stay 2-3 years — either she finds a new role or I have a promotion for her. My advice: your next job should be at an AI-native organization. Don't go somewhere worse than where you are now.

Then the uncomfortable implication: if you can't do AI-native workflow without me as your manager — if you need me to build the skills, troubleshoot the setup, assign the tasks — then you can't get hired at an AI-native product org. And if you can find a job that doesn't require AI-native skills, that means it's not an AI-native org, and it's probably not worth your time.

We agreed. That conversation is the version of "share the why" that actually works. Not "the industry is changing" (abstract). Not "companies are cutting 40%" (scare tactic). But: "This skill is the difference between your next role being a step up or a step down. I care about your career, and this is what the career market is going to demand."

This is the framing I'd use with the other PMs too. It's personal, honest, and grounded in their specific situation — not in articles about other companies.

**What I should have said months ago:** "The standard for what a PM delivers is changing. Our leadership is aligned on this direction. PMs who can do their own data analysis and build demos are more effective, and that's becoming the expectation. If you want your next role to be a step up, this is the skill that gets you there."

### The pattern across all four layers

Each layer is a version of choosing comfort over clarity. I stayed in groundwork mode because it was safe. I inspired instead of setting expectations. I avoided the expectation because it leads to performance conversations. I withheld the context that would have made the expectation make sense. The insight isn't that these were always wrong — Layer 0 was genuinely necessary, and some of the caution in Layer 3 may have been appropriate before the evidence was in. The insight is that I didn't recognize the transition points.

## What I Now Believe

_These are observations from my team, not a universal playbook. They might apply to your team. They might not._

### The groundwork phase is real — but it has an expiration date

You can't set expectations you haven't validated. The months I spent proving AI-assisted PM work delivers were necessary — both to build evidence and to build the expertise to support others. But the groundwork phase can become a hiding place. At some point, the evidence is sufficient and continuing to "prove it" is just procrastination.

### This playbook requires doing the work yourself first

I did 400 messages in 43 sessions in a week. I built the SEO skill the PM now uses. I can troubleshoot when someone gets stuck because I've hit the same walls. The "set expectations + support" formula depends on the quality of the support — and the quality of the support depends on the leader having done the obsessive groundwork. A leader who copies the playbook without doing the work will get the expectations part right and fail at the support part.

I don't encourage other leaders to copy this if they're not ready to do the real work themselves. That's my leadership style — I can't ask people to do something I haven't done. Some leaders operate differently. I respect that. It's just not how I work.

### Inspiration works for people who already have the drive

The data lead. The junior PM. For these people, my job is simple: get out of the way, remove setup barriers, 30 minutes of pairing at most.

### For everyone else, you need three things

1. **The why:** Our leadership is aligned that this is the direction. This isn't my hobby — this is where the company is going.
2. **The expectation:** PMs deliver data-backed analysis and demos. The tool doesn't matter — the output quality does.
3. **The support:** Pairing sessions on their actual work. Structural changes like moving workflows to GitHub where AI can reach the context. Not demos of my work — help with theirs.

### I haven't tested the expectation-setting yet

I'm being honest: I haven't set the expectation or shared the leadership context. This lesson is a pivot point, not a proven conclusion. The evidence I have: inspiration alone didn't work, forcing worked once (and I can't explain why), pairing worked for someone who was already eager. The hypothesis: clear expectations with honest context and real support will work more broadly. One data point isn't proof. But zero data points supported the inspiration approach.

The difference between now and six months ago: I've done the groundwork. I have concrete examples of what good looks like. I can point to the PM's SEO report, my own project output, the junior PM's adoption. The expectation isn't theoretical — it's backed by proof that it's achievable.

I'll know I'm right if, in 4 weeks, PMs who haven't been using AI tools are delivering analysis they couldn't have done manually. I'll know I'm wrong if I set the expectation and nothing changes.

## What I'm Still Figuring Out

### What "support" actually means

For the PM, support was assigning a specific task. For the junior PM, it was 30 minutes of setup. For the people who didn't adopt, support was walkthroughs that didn't work. "Support" means different things for different people and I don't have a taxonomy yet.

### The performance conversation I haven't had

If AI-assisted analysis becomes a PM job expectation, what happens when someone can't meet it? I've been avoiding this by framing adoption as optional. Making it an expectation means being willing to have that harder conversation.

### Whether the PM's role is actually changing

I believe the PM's core job is: (1) decide what to build by getting the data to support the claim and aligning with management, and (2) get things done. Most PMs focus on (2) and forget (1). AI changes (1) dramatically — a PM with AI tools can do data analysis, build demos, and test APIs that used to require other people. That raises the bar for what "good" looks like.

---

## The Lesson

I spent months trying to inspire AI adoption. The team thought it was my hobby. The one person I forced is the strongest adopter — though I can't fully explain why it worked for her and not others.

The months weren't wasted. I was building the evidence that this way of working delivers, and building the expertise to support others through the transition. I couldn't set an expectation I hadn't validated, and I couldn't support people through adoption without having done the work myself. But I didn't recognize when the groundwork was done. I stayed in proving-it mode because it was comfortable.

Four layers of choosing comfort over clarity — some justified at the time, some not. The insight isn't that I was always wrong. It's that I didn't recognize the transition points: when groundwork becomes procrastination, when inspiration becomes avoidance, when protecting people from context becomes withholding the reason they need to change.

The fix: share why our leadership is aligned on this direction, set the expectation for output quality, provide real support grounded in my own experience. Each step requires being the clear leader instead of the comfortable one.

I'm about to test this. I'll let you know how it goes.
