# The Team Thought AI Was My Hobby

*Four layers of choosing comfort over clarity when trying to get my team to use AI*

---

Over lunch, the one PM who'd actually adopted AI on my team told me something I didn't want to hear: the rest of the team thinks it's my hobby.

She was right. I'd spent months running demos, walking people through my workflow, making my output visible. I was convinced this was how PMs should work now. Most of my team nodded politely and went back to their old workflow. I thought they were unmotivated. They thought I was enthusiastic about a personal interest.

I'd been solving the wrong problem. Not adoption. Leadership.

---

## What I tried

Claude Code is a command-line AI tool that can read your codebase, edit files, and execute multi-step tasks. I used it for everything — data analysis, product demos, automated reports. I showed people what it could do. Walked them through it multiple times. Built demos, made my own work visible. Nobody adopted.

I forced one PM to run a monthly SEO analysis through Claude Code. She had no coding experience. She now generates a full interactive report on her own — the strongest adopter on the team. But I forced other PMs with equally concrete, repeatable tasks, and they didn't adopt. The tasks weren't the difference. Something about her disposition was. I still can't explain it. One data point isn't a method.

A junior PM with a QA background had been eager to try for months but hadn't started. Thirty minutes of pairing — showing him the setup, helping him configure his first tools. He's now using it proactively. But the 30 minutes removed a setup barrier, not a motivation barrier. He was already there.

The data team lead built a daily automated analysis pipeline on a Raspberry Pi. Nobody assigned it. He found his own problem and solved it. He also challenged my approach to building tools that hide AI from users: "That doesn't help the team learn." He had a point.

Others I didn't push — a senior PM whose real problem isn't AI adoption, marketing who'd never seen VS Code, engineers at 7% AI-assisted coding beyond tab completion. Different problems, different dynamics, not this story.

---

## What I got wrong

I want to describe this as four mistakes, but that's not quite right. It's four layers — each one peeling back a deeper version of the same instinct. Some of them were the right call at the time. The insight isn't that I was always wrong. It's that I didn't recognize when they stopped being correct.

**The groundwork wasn't wasted.** I couldn't have set expectations on day one. I hadn't proven to myself that AI-assisted PM work actually delivers. The PM's SEO report, the product demos, the API-grounded specs — that was the evidence base. If that work hadn't produced real results, I had no business asking anyone else to do it. And the groundwork wasn't just about evidence. It built the expertise I need to actually support someone when they get stuck. I can troubleshoot when things break, build the tools they need, demonstrate what "good" looks like — because I've done the obsessive work myself. I can't ask people to do something I haven't done. That's deliberate, not a caveat.

The mistake was not recognizing when the groundwork was done. Proving it became a hiding place. The evidence was sufficient months ago. I kept building more because building is comfortable.

**Once the evidence was in, I kept doing demos instead of setting expectations.** I walked people through walkthroughs. I made my own work visible. The implicit pitch: *look how powerful this is — you should want to use it.* That works for self-starters who just need barriers removed. The data lead found his own problem and solved it without me. The junior PM needed 30 minutes of setup, not motivation. For everyone else, demos produced polite nods.

A demo lets you be the inspiring leader. An expectation makes you accountable for the outcome. I chose the demo every time.

**Setting the expectation would have turned adoption into a performance problem.** If I say "all PMs must deliver analysis and demos through AI," then I own what happens when someone can't. Or won't. That's a harder conversation than any walkthrough. I wasn't ready for it. I told myself I was being patient. I was being comfortable.

**Then the PM told me what the team actually thought.** She said the team sees AI adoption as my personal agenda — mine and the data lead's. They think we push it because we enjoy it. She was right. Our CEO, COO, and CTO are all aligned that AI-assisted work is the direction for the company. I'd never told the team that. I kept the leadership alignment and the industry pressure to myself because I didn't want to scare people. Without the "why," every demo and walkthrough reinforced exactly the narrative she described: *this is Derek's hobby.*

They were being polite, not resistant. I'd mistaken courtesy for a motivation problem.

At that same lunch, we talked about her career trajectory. She'd stay two, maybe three years — either I have a promotion for her or she finds her next role. My advice: your next job should be at an AI-native organization. Don't go somewhere worse than where you are now.

Then the uncomfortable implication landed. If she can't do AI-native work without me as her manager — if she needs me to build the skills, troubleshoot the setup, assign the tasks — she can't get hired at an AI-native product org. And if she can find a job that doesn't require those skills, that job is probably not worth her time. We both knew it.

That conversation is the version of "share the why" that actually works. Not "the industry is changing" — that's abstract. Not "companies are cutting headcount" — that's a scare tactic. But: *this skill is the difference between your next role being a step up or a step down.* Personal. Career-grounded. Honest. I'm aware that coming from a manager, this lands partly as pressure. I can't fully separate the advice from the authority. But I'd rather be honest about where the industry is going than comfortable about it. This is the framing I'd use with the other PMs too.

The pattern across all four layers is the same instinct: choosing comfort over clarity. Staying in groundwork mode because it was safe. Inspiring instead of expecting. Avoiding the expectation because it leads to performance conversations. Withholding the context that would have made the expectation make sense. Each one felt reasonable in the moment. Some of them *were* reasonable — the groundwork was genuinely necessary, and some caution about sharing the leadership context made sense before the evidence was in. But I didn't recognize the transition points. I didn't notice when necessary caution became convenient avoidance.

---

## What I now believe

*These are observations from one small team, not a universal playbook.*

The groundwork phase is real. You can't set expectations you haven't validated, and you can't support people through adoption without having done the obsessive work yourself. But the groundwork phase has an expiration date, and mine had passed. At some point, continuing to "prove it" is just procrastination disguised as diligence. This playbook only works for leaders willing to do the real work first — that's deliberate, not a caveat.

For self-starters — the data lead who built an automated analysis pipeline on a Raspberry Pi without being asked, the junior PM who'd been eager to try for months — my job is simple: get out of their way. Thirty minutes of pairing to remove a setup barrier, then step back. These people don't need inspiration. They need you to not be an obstacle.

For everyone else, here's what I'm going to try: share the why — personal and career-grounded, not abstract. Set the expectation around output quality, not tool choice. And provide real support — pairing on their actual tasks, not demos of mine.

I haven't tested this yet. This lesson is a pivot point, not a proven conclusion. Inspiration alone produced polite nods. Forcing worked once, for reasons I can't explain. Pairing worked for someone who was already motivated. Zero data points supported continuing the inspiration approach. The hypothesis is directionally better, but it's still a hypothesis.

---

## What I'm still figuring out

I don't have a taxonomy for what "support" means. For one PM, support was assigning a specific task and not letting her quit. For the junior PM, it was 30 minutes of setup help. For the people who didn't adopt, support was walkthroughs — and walkthroughs didn't work. Support meant something different for each person, and I can't yet articulate the pattern that determines which kind someone needs.

Making AI-assisted work a job expectation means being willing to have the performance conversation when someone can't meet it. I've been avoiding this by keeping adoption optional. I haven't had that harder conversation yet, and I'm not sure I know how to have it well. Setting the expectation without being prepared for that follow-through is just a different version of choosing comfort.

There's also a question I haven't addressed: what happens when AI-assisted output is wrong? If I'm raising the bar for PM output quality, I need a story for verification too. The first time someone ships an AI-generated analysis with subtly wrong numbers, trust in the whole approach collapses. I don't have that answer yet.

And underneath all of this: AI is changing what a PM can do alone. Data analysis, working demos, API testing — work that used to require other people. That raises the bar for what "good" looks like, and not everyone will clear it. I need to be honest about that too.

I'm about to test this. I'll let you know how it goes.
